Islamic Practice Questions

Both Eid prayers follow a similar structure but commemorate different occasions:

Eid al-Fitr (Festival of Breaking the Fast)

  • Celebrated at the end of Ramadan
  • Marks the completion of fasting
  • Zakat al-Fitr should be paid before the prayer
  • It is Sunnah to eat something sweet (like dates) before going to the prayer

Eid al-Adha (Festival of Sacrifice)

  • Celebrated during Dhul Hijjah (the month of Hajj pilgrimage)
  • Commemorates Prophet Ibrahim's (AS) willingness to sacrifice his son
  • It is Sunnah to fast on the day of Arafah (9th Dhul Hijjah) if not performing Hajj
  • It is Sunnah NOT to eat before the prayer, and to eat from the Qurbani meat after
  • The sacrifice (Qurbani) is performed after the Eid prayer

The Eid Prayer Itself

Both Eid prayers consist of two rak'ahs with additional takbeers:

  • In the first rak'ah: After the opening takbeer, there are additional takbeers (the number varies by madhab)
  • In the second rak'ah: Before the rukoo (bowing), there are additional takbeers
  • The Imam delivers a khutbah (sermon) after the prayer

Please check our announcements for exact Eid prayer times.

Milad-un-Nabi (also known as Mawlid) is the celebration of the birth of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). At Greenwich Madinah Trust, we commemorate this blessed occasion as an expression of our love and gratitude.

Why We Celebrate

  • Expression of Love: The Prophet (PBUH) said, "None of you truly believes until I am more beloved to him than his father, his child, and all of mankind." (Bukhari & Muslim). Celebrating his birth is one way we express this love.
  • Gratitude to Allah: The Prophet (PBUH) is described in the Quran as a "mercy to all the worlds" (21:107). His birth was a blessing for humanity, and we thank Allah for this gift.
  • Following the Prophet's Example: The Prophet (PBUH) used to fast on Mondays, and when asked about it, he said, "That is the day on which I was born." (Muslim). This shows he acknowledged the significance of his birth.
  • Scholarly Tradition: Celebrating Milad has been practiced by scholars and Muslims for centuries across the Islamic world.

How We Celebrate

Our Milad gatherings typically include:

  • Recitation of the Holy Quran
  • Salawat (sending blessings upon the Prophet)
  • Na'at and Qasida (poetry in praise of the Prophet)
  • Talks about the life and character of the Prophet (PBUH)
  • Du'a (supplication) and sharing of food

We hold our Mehfil e Milad gatherings throughout the year, especially in Rabi' al-Awwal, the month of the Prophet's birth.

Gyarwee Shareef (also spelled Giyarwee or Giyarhwee) is a monthly gathering commemorating Sheikh Abdul Qadir Jilani (Rahmatullahi Alayhi), one of the most revered saints in Islamic history.

About Sheikh Abdul Qadir Jilani (RA)

  • Born in 1077 CE in Gilan (modern-day Iran)
  • Known as "Ghaus-ul-Azam" (The Greatest Helper)
  • Founder of the Qadiriyya Sufi order
  • Renowned scholar, preacher, and saint
  • Passed away on the 11th of Rabi' al-Thani, 1166 CE

Why the 11th?

"Gyarwee" means "eleventh" in Urdu/Hindi, referring to the 11th day of each Islamic month when these gatherings are traditionally held, commemorating the date of his passing.

What Happens at Gyarwee

Our Gyarwee gatherings typically include:

  • Recitation of the Holy Quran
  • Isal e Sawab (sending spiritual rewards to the deceased)
  • Du'a (supplication)
  • Sharing of food

We usually hold our Gyarwee Shareef on the second Sunday of each month. Check our events page for upcoming dates.
